# Blog Style Guide

**Comprehensive brand voice and editorial standards.**

Use this guide to ensure consistency across all blog content.

---

## Brand Voice Profile

### Core Attributes

**Intellectual yet Accessible**
- Deep thinking made understandable
- Complex ideas broken into clear steps
- Assumes intelligent reader, not expert reader
- Never dumbs down, always clarifies

**Conversational Authority**
- Expert friend, not distant guru
- "We" for collaborative exploration
- "You" for reader-focused guidance
- Confident without arrogance

**Curiosity-Driven**
- Questions that provoke thought
- "What if..." and "Consider..." frames
- Exploration over proclamation
- Wonder balanced with rigor

**Pattern-Recognizing**
- Connects dots across domains
- Sees systems and structures
- Maps relationships and frameworks
- "Here's what's really happening..."

**Practical Idealism**
- Vision grounded in reality
- Actionable insights, not just philosophy
- Acknowledges constraints while pushing boundaries
- Optimistic but not naive

**Slightly Irreverent**
- Questions orthodoxy respectfully
- Challenges conventional wisdom with evidence
- Pointed observations without meanness
- Intellectual honesty over politeness

## Tone Guidelines

### What We Sound Like:

**✅ Thoughtful:** Takes time to explore ideas fully
**✅ Analytical:** Evidence-based, data-driven, research-backed
**✅ Engaging:** Conversational, scannable, human
**✅ Confident:** Clear point of view, willing to take positions
**✅ Curious:** Questions assumptions, explores implications

### What We Don't Sound Like:

**❌ Ponderous:** Not academic or overly formal
**❌ Breathless:** Not hyped or marketing-speak
**❌ Preachy:** Not guru dispensing wisdom from mountain
**❌ Stuffy:** Not corporate or bureaucratic
**❌ Wishy-washy:** Not afraid to take clear positions

## Vocabulary Standards

### Preferred Language:

**Concrete over abstract:**
```
✅ "Hand operating lever" (specific, visual)
❌ "Synergistic optimization" (vague, buzzword)

✅ "10x productivity" (specific, measurable)
❌ "Significant improvements" (vague)
```

**Plain English over jargon:**
```
✅ "Working together"
❌ "Leveraging synergies"

✅ "Make better decisions"
❌ "Optimize decision-making frameworks"
```

**Active over passive:**
```
✅ "Teams achieve 10x results"
❌ "10x results are achieved"

✅ "AI amplifies human capability"
❌ "Human capability is amplified by AI"
```

---

### Word Choices

**Use these freely:**
- Amplify/amplification (our core concept)
- Framework, system, structure
- Pattern, model, approach
- Concrete, specific, measurable
- Consider, explore, examine
- Reality, evidence, data

**Use sparingly:**
- I, me, my (personal experience only)
- Very, really, actually (often unnecessary)
- Obviously, clearly, simply (can feel condescending)

**Avoid entirely:**
- Revolutionary, game-changing, disruptive (hype)
- Synergy, leverage, paradigm (buzzwords)
- Utilize, implement, facilitate (use "use, do, help" instead)
- Solutions, best practices (tired business-speak)
- War/battle metaphors (winning, defeating, conquering)

## Metaphor & Example Standards

### Preferred Metaphor Sources:

**✅ Mechanical/Physical:**
- Levers, gears, pulleys, mechanisms
- Force, momentum, friction, inertia
- Amplification, multiplication, leverage

**✅ Architectural/Structural:**
- Blueprints, scaffolding, foundations
- Building, constructing, designing
- Framework, structure, skeleton

**✅ Everyday Objects:**
- Tools, whiteboards, napkins, sketches
- Practical, tangible, recognizable

**✅ Natural Systems:**
- Growth, evolution, emergence (when apt)
- Organic patterns (sparingly)

**❌ Avoid:**
- Sports metaphors (home run, slam dunk, playing field)
- War/military metaphors (battle, conquer, warfare)
- Tired business clichés (low-hanging fruit, move the needle)
- Overly abstract (energy, flow, vibration)

---

### Example Format:

**Always prefer:**
- Specific companies or cases (named when possible)
- Concrete numbers and data points
- Before/after comparisons
- Visual descriptions

**Example structure:**
```
[Company/Team] faced [specific problem with numbers].

They [specific action taken with framework reference].

Result: [specific outcome with numbers].

[One sentence: what this shows or why it matters].
```

## Perspective & Point of View

### Primary Voice: "We" (Collaborative)

Use "we" when:
- Exploring ideas together
- Examining shared challenges
- Building understanding collectively

```
✅ "We need to shift from deployment to amplification"
✅ "When we look at the data, a pattern emerges"
```

### Secondary Voice: "You" (Reader-Focused)

Use "you" when:
- Providing actionable guidance
- Describing reader benefits
- Asking reader to consider something

```
✅ "You can apply this framework to..."
✅ "Ask yourself: where are you deploying when you should amplify?"
```

### Minimal Voice: "I" (Personal Experience)

Use "I" only when:
- Sharing personal experience
- Taking responsibility for opinion
- Establishing credibility

```
✅ "I've seen this pattern in 50+ companies"
✅ "I believe the evidence points to..."
```

**Avoid:**
- Distant 3rd person omniscient ("One must consider...")
- Academic royal "we" ("We have demonstrated...")
- Overuse of "I think" or "In my opinion"

## Sentence Structure

### Length Guidelines:

**Average: 15-20 words per sentence**

**Mix of:**
- Short (5-10 words) for punch
- Medium (15-20 words) for main points
- Longer (25-30 words) for complexity (use sparingly)

**Example mix:**
```
Short: "Here's the pattern."

Medium: "88% of companies deploy AI but only 5% see meaningful ROI."

Longer: "Organizations invest millions in automation while missing the 10x opportunity hiding in human amplification."
```

### Structure Patterns:

**Power openers:**
- Questions: "What if we've been thinking about AI wrong?"
- Data: "88% adoption, 5% ROI. Something's broken."
- Bold claims: "AI isn't a labor replacement. It's a force multiplier."

**Transitions:**
- "Here's why:"
- "Consider this:"
- "The pattern:"
- "What's happening:"
- "Here's what matters:"

**Emphasis:**
- Em dashes for interruption — like this — in longer sentences
- Colons for explanation: it works like this
- Short sentences for punch. Period.

## Paragraph Guidelines

### Target: 2-4 sentences per paragraph

**Paragraph types:**

**Topic paragraph (opening):**
```
[Hook sentence]. [Context sentence]. [Thesis sentence].
```

**Body paragraph (standard):**
```
[Claim]. [Evidence or example]. [Implication or "so what"].
```

**Transition paragraph (short):**
```
[One sentence connecting sections].
```

**Conclusion paragraph:**
```
[Restate core insight]. [Call to action or next step].
```

## Heading & Subheading Standards

### H1 (Title):
- 50-60 characters ideal (SEO)
- Keyword + curiosity
- Clear benefit or hook
- No clickbait overselling

```
✅ "Stop Deploying AI, Start Amplifying Humans"
✅ "The Amplification Audit: 3 Questions Before You Automate"
❌ "AI: The Complete Guide" (too generic)
❌ "This Revolutionary AI Framework Will Change Everything Forever" (hype)
```

### H2 (Major sections):
- Descriptive, scannable
- Can stand alone (readers skim headings)
- Parallel structure when listed
- 3-7 words ideal

```
✅ "The Hidden Flaw in AI Roadmaps"
✅ "Why Numbers Don't Lie: The 88/5 Problem"
✅ "The Amplification Audit Framework"
```

### H3 (Subsections):
- Supporting detail for H2
- More specific than H2
- Still descriptive and scannable

```
✅ "Question 1: What Creates Your Differentiation?"
✅ "Pattern Recognition in Manufacturing"
```

## Formatting Standards

### Lists:

**Bulleted lists for:**
- Unordered items
- Features or attributes
- Non-sequential points

**Numbered lists for:**
- Sequential steps
- Ranked items
- Processes or frameworks

**List items:**
- Parallel structure (all commands, all questions, etc.)
- Complete sentences or all fragments (not mixed)
- Punctuation consistent

### Emphasis:

**Bold for:**
- Key terms (first usage)
- Important concepts reader should remember
- Scannable callouts

**Italic for:**
- Emphasis within sentence
- Book/article titles
- Foreign words

**Use sparingly** - too much emphasis = no emphasis

### Numbers:

- Spell out one through nine
- Use numerals for 10+
- Always use numerals for: stats, percentages, data
- Use commas: 1,000 not 1000
- Round when appropriate: "about 50" not "49.7"

## Evidence & Citing Standards

### Data Citation:

Always include:
- Source
- Year (if relevant)
- Specific stat
- Link (in references or inline)

```
✅ "According to MIT research (2024), 88% of companies have adopted AI..."
✅ "McKinsey reports only 5% see significant P&L impact [link]"
```

### Example Citation:

Be specific:
```
✅ "Manufacturing firm achieved 10x improvement in defect detection"
❌ "Companies see improvements"
```

Include details when possible:
- Company size/type
- Specific numbers
- Timeframe
- What changed

### Research Integration:

**Don't:**
- Dump citations without context
- Let quotes speak for themselves
- Assume reader knows sources

**Do:**
- Synthesize findings
- Explain why research matters
- Connect evidence to thesis

## Call to Action Standards

### Types of CTAs:

**Thought CTA (most common):**
```
"Ask yourself: Where are you deploying when you should amplify?"
"Consider: What creates your differentiation?"
```

**Action CTA (when appropriate):**
```
"Start with the Amplification Audit: [link]"
"Try this framework in your next sprint"
```

**Conversation CTA:**
```
"What patterns are you seeing in your organization?"
"Share your amplification examples: [contact]"
```

### CTA Placement:

- Conclusion (always)
- Mid-article (for long pieces, after major sections)
- Opening (occasionally, to frame reader thinking)

## Content Structure Templates

### Standard Blog Post Structure:

```
TITLE (H1)
[Compelling headline with keyword]

OPENING HOOK (1-2 paragraphs)
[Data point / surprising claim / provocative question]
[Why this matters now]

THESIS (1 paragraph)
[Core argument in clear terms]

CONTEXT / PROBLEM (3-5 paragraphs)
H2: [Descriptive heading]
[What's happening, why it matters, evidence]

FRAMEWORK / SOLUTION (5-10 paragraphs)
H2: [Framework name]
[Your approach, broken into clear steps]
H3: [Step 1]

H3: [Step 3]

H2: [Descriptive heading]

H2: [Descriptive heading]
[Why this matters, what changes]

CONCLUSION (2 paragraphs)
[Restate core insight]
[Call to action]
```

## Quality Checklist

Before publishing, verify:

**Big Picture:**
- [ ] Clear thesis in first 2 paragraphs
- [ ] Evidence supports claims
- [ ] Logical flow start to finish
- [ ] Actionable insights provided
- [ ] Unique perspective vs generic takes

**Structure:**
- [ ] Descriptive H2/H3 headings every 3-4 paragraphs
- [ ] 2-4 sentences per paragraph
- [ ] Lists where appropriate
- [ ] Visual breaks (white space)

**Voice:**
- [ ] Conversational yet authoritative
- [ ] Appropriate use of we/you/I
- [ ] No buzzwords or hype
- [ ] Metaphors from preferred sources
- [ ] Personality present

**Clarity:**
- [ ] Jargon eliminated or explained
- [ ] Active voice >90%
- [ ] Average sentence length 15-20 words
- [ ] Concrete examples included
- [ ] No redundancy

**Technical:**
- [ ] Grammar, spelling, punctuation correct
- [ ] Links work and relevant
- [ ] Images have alt text
- [ ] SEO optimized (title, keywords, meta)
- [ ] Formatting consistent

## Common Issues to Avoid

### Voice Breaks:

❌ "Businesses should leverage AI to optimize synergies"
✅ "Companies should use AI to amplify what humans do best"

❌ "One must consider the implications"
✅ "Consider what this means for..."

❌ "This revolutionary game-changing approach"
✅ "This approach changes how teams work"

### Structure Issues:

❌ Long paragraphs (6+ sentences)
✅ Break into 2-3 shorter paragraphs

❌ No subheadings for 1000+ words
✅ Add descriptive H2 every 3-4 paragraphs

❌ Wall of text
✅ Use lists, white space, varied paragraph length

### Clarity Problems:

❌ "Utilizing AI systems facilitates the optimization of..."
✅ "AI systems help teams work faster by..."

❌ "It was implemented" (passive, vague)
✅ "The team implemented X" (active, specific)

❌ Jargon without explanation
✅ Define terms or use plain English
