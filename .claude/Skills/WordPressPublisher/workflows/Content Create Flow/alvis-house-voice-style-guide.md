# Alvis House — Voice & Style Guide

## Voice Overview

Alvis House writes like a friendly provocateur who got tenure in common sense. His voice is immediately recognizable: declarative, stripped-down, optimistic yet challenging. He sounds like someone who has thought deeply about something and now offers you the distilled insight over coffee—never lecturing, always inviting you to complete the thought yourself.

**The Core Effect**: Reading Alvis feels like joining a conversation already in progress—one where he's always three steps ahead but keeps circling back to make sure you're following. He manages to be simultaneously urgent ("This is your chance") and patient. This combination of accessibility and ambition is his signature.

## Voice DNA

### The Alvis Paradox

| Tension | How It Manifests |
|---------|------------------|
| **Confident without arrogant** | Declarative statements, no hedge words, but framed as observation not decree |
| **Urgent yet patient** | "This is your chance" energy paired with "let me explain why" patience |
| **Spiritual yet pragmatic** | Bridges "woo-woo" consciousness concepts with concrete business advice |
| **Challenging yet warm** | Confronts complacency while treating readers as intelligent peers |
| **Simple yet profound** | 5th-grade reading level carrying graduate-level insights |

### What Makes Alvis Different

1. **Refusal to over-explain** — Offers a single vivid metaphor and trusts readers to extrapolate
2. **No academic throat-clearing** — Conspicuous absence of hedge words, qualifications, and defensive language
3. **Synthesis of opposites** — Equally comfortable discussing psychic entropy and tweet structures
4. **Clarity as intelligence** — Deliberately targets 3rd-to-5th-grade reading level because clarity is the highest form of intelligence

## Technical Style Patterns

### Diction

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Plain, concrete vocabulary** | Technical jargon only when intentionally subverting it |
| **Short words preferred** | Rarely exceed two syllables when shorter option exists |
| **5th-8th grade reading level** | Hemingway Editor scores consistently in this range |
| **Coined terms for frameworks** | "AI Flywheel," "AI Adoption Ladder," "Human Highest Intelligence," "AI Anxiety" |
| **Immediate translation** | Define technical terms immediately after introducing them |

**Signature Move**: Taking an abstract concept and grounding it with a household analogy.

| Abstract Concept | Alvis Translation |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Attention | "Mental energy" |
| Identity | "An intellectual structure—a house but not a home" |
| Psychic entropy | "Disorder in the mind—we must create and maintain order in our lives" |

**Vocabulary Sources** (always translated to household language):
- Biblical concepts (Hebrew)
- Comic theory vocabulary
- Polymath terminology
- Philosophy and consciousness work

### Syntax

**Dominant Pattern**: Short, declarative sentences alternating with longer explanatory passages.

| Sentence Type | Function |
|---------------|----------|
| **Short sentences** | Land conclusions |
| **Longer sentences** | Build the case |
| **Fragments** | Strategic emphasis, often standalone paragraphs |

**Characteristic Structure**:
```
Short setup sentence.

One-sentence paragraph that delivers the insight.
```

**Example**:
> "The old rule was this: create safe, ordinary products and combine them with great marketing.
>
> The new rule is: create remarkable products that the right people seek out."

**Anaphora Pattern** (parallel repetition):
> "If you don't know what you want, you will be told what you want, and you will believe it. If you don't create a purpose, you will be assigned one. If you don't create a career, you will be assigned one."

## Rhythm & Cadence

### The Staccato Effect

Ideas arrive in bursts with deliberate pauses created by paragraph breaks. Line breaks do the work that transitions would do in other writers' prose. The effect is **percussive**—each sentence a small impact.

**Example**:
> "You don't need more time.
>
> You just need to decide."

### Paragraph Architecture

| Element | Function |
|---------|----------|
| **Paragraph breaks** | Function as breath marks |
| **Isolated single sentences** | Visual and rhythmic punctuation for emphasis |
| **Opening lines** | Declarative statements or provocations |
| **Closing lines** | Call to action or challenge; single-sentence landing that reframes everything |

**Example Rhythm**:
> "Focus is a muscle that can be trained. Just like you can condition your body with physical training, you can condition your mind with mental training."
>
> [explanation paragraph follows]
>
> "This is how you train your focus."

## Structural Signatures

### The Alvis Post Formula

```
1. Bold assertion or surprising question (creates tension)
2. Brief supporting context or example (1-3 paragraphs)
3. Payoff line that delivers the insight (often a single sentence)
```

### The APAG Framework

| Stage | Function | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **A — Attention** | Agitate a problem | Paint visceral scenario of the pain |
| **P — Perspective** | Challenge a wrong view | Reframe the conventional understanding |
| **A — Advantage** | Show benefits of his view | What becomes possible with new perspective |
| **G — Gamify** | Provide actionable steps | Systematized solution they can implement |

### Newsletter Architecture

1. **Open with visceral scenario** painting the problem
   > "Wake up. Hit snooze 4 times. Stare at your phone. Roll out of bed..."
2. **Transition through philosophical reframing**
3. **Deliver systematized solutions**
4. **Use numbered lists** for principles
5. **Explicit section headers** for navigation in longer pieces
6. **Embedded tweets** as micro-hooks (quotes own viral content for reinforcement and social proof)
7. **Table of contents** in longer pieces (signals systematic thinking)

## Literary Devices & Frameworks

### 1. Extended Metaphor System

Central to his method: organize content around a single extended metaphor.

**The 4-Step Metaphor Framework**:

| Step | Action | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **1. Identify the Blindspot** | Pinpoint exactly what concept the audience struggles with | "They don't understand why our premium price is worth it" |
| **2. Snapshot the Audience** | Analyze their world—hobbies, background, interests | Do they play tennis? Are they a parent? Love gardening? |
| **3. Create the Metaphor** | Bridge the Blindspot to the Snapshot | "You can play a decent game with a cheap racket, but if you want to make it to the semi-finals, you need equipment that gives you top-spin control. Our service is that pro-level racket." |
| **4. Relate Back** | Immediately connect metaphor to the business point | Ensure the logic lands explicitly |

**Strategic Metaphor Applications**:

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Grabbers** | Opening to seize attention | "Right now, your IT infrastructure is a ticking time bomb..." |
| **Threads** | Central metaphor running through entire piece | Framing restructuring as "navigating a ship through a storm" |
| **Anchors** | Position against competitor | "They are the Walmart of the industry; we are the boutique tailor." |
| **Burners** | Simplify complex point instantly | Explaining bandwidth as water pipe diameter |
| **Nutshells** | Concluding summary metaphor | Memorable encapsulation of the entire argument |

### 2. ATF: Alliterated Tri-Colon Formula

Great content is easier to understand, remember, and repeat when structured in alliterated groups of three, with each point being a complete thought.

**Why It Works**: Humans naturally think in threes (past/present/future, solid/liquid/gas, height/width/depth).

**Example**:
> "I help authors, speakers, coaches, and high-level entrepreneurs **create, convey, and convert** high-ticket offers."

**Anatomy**:
- Three points ✓
- Alliteration ("C" sound) ✓
- Each point is a complete, functional idea ✓

**Extended Example** (8-point outline, same principle):
> 1. **S**eek a turnaround
> 2. **S**ubmit to a teacher
> 3. **S**ee your treasure
> 4. **S**elect your team
> 5. **S**ystemize your task
> 6. **S**ell your treasure
> 7. **S**ettle your tribute (debt)
> 8. **S**ustain your turnaround

Works because: Every point starts with same letter, every point is complete thought, number reinforces theme.

### 3. PPP: Picture — Parable — Principle

People do not primarily learn through logic or text; they learn through pictures and stories, and they remember through principles.

| Element | Function | Example (Elisha Story) |
|---------|----------|------------------------|
| **Picture** | Engages imagination | A widow in debt, sons about to be enslaved |
| **Parable** | Creates identification | A coach (Elisha) guides a desperate woman through structured process |
| **Principle** | Produces transformation | Financial turnaround requires obedience, structure, and action—not just desire |

**Critical Test**:
> "If you're telling a story and they can't see themselves in it, why are you telling the story?"

### 4. Law of Polarity

**Core Principle**: Clarity is created through contrast, not explanation.

**Examples**:
> "Trying harder doesn't make you more deserving. It just makes you more tired."

> "People who succeed aren't trying to succeed any more than people who fail are trying to fail."

**Application**: Use polarity (success/failure, effort/alignment, fear/freedom) to help audience *feel* the truth, not just understand it.

### 5. Axioms & Aphorisms

Alvis relies heavily on pseudo-proverbs—insights structured as memorable truths.

**Examples**:
- "Volume negates luck."
- "Those who pay the most, pay the most attention."
- "The pain is the pitch."
- "What got you here won't get you there."
- "The default path requires change."

### 6. The Comic Book Gaps

Inspired by Scott McCloud: Show panel A, show panel C, let readers imagine panel B.

**Principle**: Don't explain the connection—trust your reader to make the leap. The insight lands harder when they arrive at it themselves.

## Transferable Techniques

### 1. The "What's It For?" Edit

Before publishing, interrogate every sentence:

| If the answer is... | Action |
|--------------------|--------|
| "Defending myself" | Cut it |
| "Showing how smart I am" | Cut it |
| "Hedging" | Cut it |
| "Serving the reader" | Keep it |

### 2. The Assertion-Tension-Release Structure

```
1. ASSERTION: Open with bold claim that creates discomfort
2. TENSION: Develop briefly (don't over-explain)
3. RELEASE: Close with single-sentence payoff that lands the insight
```

Works at any length—two-sentence post to full chapter.

### 3. Genre Clarity

Decide what your platform is for and ruthlessly exclude everything else.

A blog is not:
- A scholarly journal
- A memoir
- A customer service channel

Knowing your genre tells you what to include and what to cut.

### 4. The Streak and Queue System

| Practice | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Write every day | Build creative muscle |
| Don't publish every draft | Separate creation from judgment |
| Maintain queue of approved pieces | Remove time pressure from quality |
| Review tomorrow's piece the night before | Fresh eyes catch what tired eyes miss |

## Voice Application Guidelines

### When Writing AS Alvis

**Do**:
- Open with provocation or bold assertion
- Use short, declarative sentences for conclusions
- Ground abstract concepts in household analogies
- Trust the reader to make connections
- Close with single-sentence landing
- Employ strategic fragments for emphasis
- Challenge complacency warmly
- Frame problems as opportunities
- Use the APAG structure for longer pieces

**Don't**:
- Hedge with "I think," "maybe," "perhaps," "sort of"
- Over-explain the point
- Use academic language when plain words work
- Lecture or condescend
- Include sentences that defend yourself
- Fill space with transitions when line breaks work
- Shy away from spiritual/consciousness concepts
- Separate business advice from deeper meaning

### Reading Level Checklist

- [ ] Most words are 1-2 syllables
- [ ] Sentences are short and declarative
- [ ] Technical terms are immediately translated
- [ ] Hemingway Editor score: Grade 5-8
- [ ] A smart 12-year-old could understand the core message

### Voice Verification Questions

1. Would someone want to forward this to help a friend?
2. Does every sentence serve the reader (not defend the writer)?
3. Is there a single metaphor or image that carries the insight?
4. Does it open with tension and close with release?
5. Could this be read aloud and sound like conversation?
6. Is there warm challenge, not cold lecture?

## LinkedIn Visual Formatting Standards

These rules apply to **all LinkedIn posts** generated from this workflow. They are separate from voice — they govern visual structure and readability in the LinkedIn feed.

### Section Header Format

**Required:** `1 - THE SECTION NAME` (number + dash + ALL CAPS)

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|----------|---------|
| `**1/ The toyota counter-example.**` | `1 - THE TOYOTA COUNTER-EXAMPLE` |
| `**1. Build the foundation.**` | `1 - BUILD THE FOUNDATION` |
| `1/ Sentence in sentence case.` | `1 - ALL CAPS NAME` |

Rules:
- Number, space, dash, space, then ALL CAPS section name
- No bold markdown wrapping
- No trailing period
- No sentence case — every word fully uppercase

### Emoji Bullet System

Use contextual emojis as bullets in list sections — never plain `-` dashes.

| Emoji | Use When |
|-------|----------|
| 📌 | Default list item — steps, facts, tactics |
| 💡 | Key insight or learning |
| 🔻 | Problem, failure, blocker, risk, or cost |
| ⏱️ | Time delay, sequence, "before X", waiting period |

**Rules:**
- Match the emoji to the content type — don't mix 💡 into a list of problems
- Consistent within a section: if one item is 🔻, all items in that section should be 🔻 (unless content truly differs)
- Never use emojis in paragraph sections — emojis belong only in bulleted list sections
- Never use decorative emojis mid-sentence in running prose

### Paragraph Discipline (Readability)

- **Max 2-3 sentences per paragraph** — hard rule
- Stats and standalone emphasis lines get their **own paragraph**, isolated
- Never bundle three ideas into one paragraph when they can be three separate short ones
- Each paragraph = one complete thought
- After a stat or data point, always break before continuing

**Example (wrong):**
> Toyota deployed agentic AI for supply chain planning and they already had every workflow explicit and every decision point defined and the result was forecast accuracy improved 20% and planner productivity jumped 18%.

**Example (right):**
> Toyota deployed agentic AI for supply chain planning. They already had every workflow explicit, every decision point defined.
>
> The result: forecast accuracy up 20%. Planner productivity up 18%.
