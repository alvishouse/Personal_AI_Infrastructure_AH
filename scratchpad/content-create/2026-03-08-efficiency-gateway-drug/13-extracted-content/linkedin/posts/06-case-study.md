Booking Holdings saved $150M with AI.
Then they spent $170M more.

Most companies pocket the savings. Booking reinvested more than they saved — before the savings arrived. That decision changed their trajectory.

This is what happens when you treat efficiency as startup capital instead of the finish line. The numbers tell the story.

1/ The efficiency win was real — but it was not the play.

Booking Holdings ran a multi-year AI transformation. Workforce optimization and technology consolidation delivered $150M in cost savings in 2025.

Most CFOs would report that as margin improvement. Their CFO did something different. He explicitly modeled reinvestment alongside the savings. The plan to spend the freed capacity existed before the capacity arrived.

- $150M saved through AI efficiency
- $170M reinvested into AI capabilities
- Connected trip platform expanded
- Fintech offerings launched

The savings funded the next move. The next move built the moat.

2/ The results speak for themselves.

Gross bookings: $166B (up 10% year-over-year). Revenue: $24B (up 11%). Adjusted EBITDA: $8B+ (up 17%). Revenue grew faster than costs.

They did not treat efficiency savings as profit. They treated them as innovation capital. The efficiency was the on-ramp. The connected trip platform was the destination.

3/ JPMorgan ran the same playbook.

Their COiN platform saved 360,000 work hours annually processing loan agreements. They did not cut 180 positions. They redirected that human capacity toward developing IndexGPT — an entirely new AI-driven investment product.

The efficiency funded the innovation. The innovation created the competitive moat. Same pattern. Different industry. Same result.

4/ The company that did the opposite.

A regional services company went all-in on automation. Every process automated. Headcount dropped. Costs fell. Efficiency metrics were spectacular.

Then a competitor used the same automation tools, achieved similar savings, and invested the freed capacity into a new service line. The first company had optimized everything and innovated nothing. When the competitor started pulling customers, they had no answer.

You cannot out-automate your way to differentiation. Efficiency is a commodity. What you build with the freed capacity is the moat.

BCG confirms: companies that reinvest the dividend deliver 3.6x greater three-year shareholder return than those that pocket it.

The question is not whether to deploy AI efficiency.

It is what you build with the savings before they arrive.
