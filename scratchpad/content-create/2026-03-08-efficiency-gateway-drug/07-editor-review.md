## Editorial Report — The Lemonade Stand Lie

### Overall Assessment

This draft is structurally strong and intellectually solid — the Capacity Dividend Framework is well-named, the lemonade stand metaphor threads consistently, and the BCG research anchors the paradox with real authority. The piece is close to publication-ready but has three blocking issues: (1) the expert story section title says "How the Capacity Dividend Walks Out the Door" but the framing briefly breaks Option B discipline with "I teach the Capacity Dividend framework" — a fabricated first-person authority claim that needs Option B rewording; (2) the research section conflates a BCG study with McKinsey and HBR citations under a single "MIT/McKinsey" heading, which is citation confusion that will undermine credibility; (3) several sections fall into over-explanation and passive academic construction that bleed the Alvis voice. Fix these three and this piece publishes.

### Voice Audit

**Hedge words found:**

- Line 70: "It is not rare. It is the default. And it is why I teach the Capacity Dividend framework before any AI efficiency conversation." — "I teach" is the violation here. The voice is fine but the claim is fabricated. Cut the final sentence or reframe.
- Line 300: "Do not expect overnight revenue lift from reinvesting freed capacity. Innovation compounds. It does not spike." — This is actually solid Alvis voice. Keep.

**Over-explanation violations:**

- Lines 76-84 (Framework opening): The explanation of the productivity equation is good but the sentence "Both girls work hard. Only one builds a business." is repeated from the subtitle. First use here is correct; the subtitle repetition is the redundancy. The subtitle actually uses "Only one built a business" — slightly different. This is fine, but the metaphor payoff at the close (line 324-326) is the strongest landing and should not be pre-empted by weaker restatements mid-piece.

- Lines 86-88: "The same equation governs your AI strategy. Every efficiency win reduces the denominator. But if the numerator stays flat, productivity barely moves. And your competitor — the one raising the numerator while you shrink the denominator — is creating a gap that widens every quarter." — The logic chain is spelled out one beat too many. "And your competitor..." sentence tells the reader what to think instead of trusting them to arrive there. Cut or compress.

- Lines 223-224: "Every AI vendor leads with cost savings. Every ROI calculator measures hours saved. Every board presentation asks about efficiency gains. The entire conversation around AI has been framed by the denominator." — The anaphora pattern (Every/Every/Every) is strong Alvis. But the final explanation sentence "The entire conversation around AI has been framed by the denominator" over-explains what the reader just understood from the three examples. Cut it.

**Academic throat-clearing:**

- Line 58: "The freed capacity — hundreds of hours per month of human attention now available — had no destination. No investment thesis. No plan." — The em-dash construction here reads slightly academic. Three short sentences would hit harder: "The freed capacity had no destination. No investment thesis. No plan." (Remove the em-dash appositive.)

- Line 139: "McKinsey found that high performers are 3x more likely to have fundamentally redesigned individual workflows." — Source is slightly weak here. The draft earlier labeled this section "MIT/McKinsey" — but this McKinsey cite is not the same study as the MIT 5.7-hour finding. These are conflated. The MIT cite comes from McKinsey's own research — both should be cited cleanly as McKinsey data, or the MIT attribution needs a specific source.

**Passive/weak constructions:**

- Line 46: "Studying how mid-market companies respond to their first AI efficiency win, the same sequence keeps surfacing." — Good Option B opener but slightly passive. Sharpen: "One pattern surfaces consistently across mid-market AI engagements."

- Line 189-193: The "Consulting Pattern" example uses "What I observed:" — this is correct Option B framing but the transition "And it is why I teach the Capacity Dividend framework" bleeds into Option A. The "Consulting Pattern" section as a whole is the shakiest example in the five. It is the closest to fabricated first-person authority. Tighten to observed framing throughout.

**Complex/compound sentences to break:**

- Line 127: "JPMorgan Chase saved 360,000 work hours annually with their COiN platform. They did not cut 180 positions. They redirected that human capacity toward developing IndexGPT — an entirely new AI-driven investment product." — Good declarative rhythm. Keep.

- Line 155-157: "BCG studied 1,800 companies across industries and found a widening AI value gap. Companies they classify as 'future-built' — those pursuing innovation-core transformation — achieve 1.7x more revenue growth, 3.6x greater three-year total shareholder return, and 40% more cost savings than laggards focused on efficiency alone." — Long but the data density is appropriate here. Keep.

**Reading level concerns:**

The piece reads at approximately Grade 8-10 in sections. The framework explanation (lines 76-88) and the research section (lines 151-165) drift toward Grade 10+ due to multi-clause sentences. Targeted line breaks and sentence breaks will bring this into range.

### Authenticity Check

**Option B framing is mostly clean.** The expert story correctly uses observed framing throughout: "A distribution company," "their CEO asked," "Meanwhile, a competitor." This is proper Option B.

**One violation to fix:**

- Line 70: "And it is why I teach the Capacity Dividend framework before any AI efficiency conversation." — This is a fabricated first-person authority claim. Alvis is not established as someone who "teaches" this framework from years of practice. Reframe as pattern observation: "This pattern — the Capacity Dividend consumed rather than invested — shows up consistently enough that it has become the first conversation in any AI efficiency engagement."

- Lines 191-192: "What I observed: The companies that succeed do one thing differently at the start." and "The ones that skip this step — every single time — end up running the treadmill." — "Every single time" is a universal claim that could only come from extensive personal experience. Soften without hedging: "The ones that skip this step consistently end up running the treadmill."

No other fabricated first-person claims found. The Booking Holdings, JPMorgan, and distribution company examples are all sourced or framed correctly.

### Top 5 Required Fixes (Priority Order)

1. **Fix the "I teach" claim (line 70) and "What I observed" section (lines 187-193)** — These are the only authenticity violations. One rewrite sentence each puts the expert story fully in clean Option B framing. This is the most critical fix because it protects Alvis's credibility.

2. **Fix the citation conflation in the Research section** — The "MIT/McKinsey" heading implies both are from MIT, but the 5.7-hour stat is McKinsey's own research (McKinsey Global Institute / McKinsey Health Institute), and the HBR stat is a separate third source. Relabel the heading as "McKinsey Research" and add HBR as a distinct callout, OR source the MIT attribution specifically. Currently this looks like sloppy citation work and undermines the credibility argument the piece makes.

3. **Cut the over-explanation sentence at the end of the "Why They Believe It" myth section** — "The entire conversation around AI has been framed by the denominator" over-explains what the three-line anaphora already proved. Trust the reader. Cut it.

4. **Tighten the Expert Story opening** — "Studying how mid-market companies respond to their first AI efficiency win, the same sequence keeps surfacing" reads passive. Reopen with: "One pattern surfaces consistently across mid-market AI engagements." Sharper, more declarative, same Option B framing.

5. **Add one sentence to the hook acknowledging the social risk of the current approach** — The VP Operator's private fear is not just "no ROI" — it is "I've been measuring the wrong thing and my boss is starting to notice." One sentence in the identity filter that names this private shame deepens the ICP lock without lengthening the hook.
