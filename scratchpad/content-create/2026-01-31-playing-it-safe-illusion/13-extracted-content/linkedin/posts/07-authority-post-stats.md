80% success rate vs. 37% success rate.

Same AI initiatives. Same technologies. Wildly different outcomes.

The difference isn't budget. It's not access to better tools.

It's something you can control starting today.

---

## The Research

MIT Sloan Management Review and Boston Consulting Group studied hundreds of AI initiatives in 2024.

They found something that should fundamentally change how you think about AI risk:

→ Organizations with clear AI strategies: **80% success rate**
→ Organizations without strategy: **37% success rate**

That's not a marginal difference. That's the difference between "AI mostly works for us" and "AI mostly doesn't."

**But here's what matters:**

The organizations with 80% success rates weren't starting with bigger budgets or better technology.

They were starting with clarity about what they were trying to learn.

---

## What "Strategy" Actually Means Here

When the research says "clear AI strategy," they don't mean a 50-page document from consultants.

They mean answering three questions before starting:

**1. What are we trying to learn?**
(Not "Will this work?" but "What will this teach us?")

**2. How will we measure learning?**
(Not just ROI, but institutional knowledge gained)

**3. What will we do with what we learn?**
(How does experiment #1 inform experiment #2?)

**That's it.**

Organizations with 80% success rates treat AI initiatives as learning investments.

Organizations with 37% success rates treat AI initiatives as technology purchases.

One group is building a compounding learning curve.

The other group is just buying software and hoping it works.

---

## The Implication That Matters

If you're waiting to invest in AI until the technology is "proven," you're optimizing for the wrong thing.

The technology is already proven. The 80% success rate organizations prove that every day.

**What's not proven is whether you can learn from AI faster than your competitors.**

And that question can only be answered by starting.

---

## What This Means for "Playing It Safe"

Here's the uncomfortable truth the 80% vs. 37% stat reveals:

The "safe" approach—waiting for proven solutions, demanding guaranteed ROI, avoiding risk—produces the 37% outcome.

The "learning" approach—partnering early, accepting failures as tuition, optimizing for institutional knowledge—produces the 80% outcome.

**The cautious path is the risky path.**

---

## The One Change You Can Make

If you're planning AI initiatives, add one question to your evaluation criteria:

**"What will we learn if this fails?"**

If the answer is "nothing" or "that this vendor isn't good," don't fund it.

If the answer is "we'll learn how our data quality affects AI performance" or "we'll learn which teams need change management support," that's a learning investment worth making.

**Because here's what the 80% organizations understand:**

AI initiatives that "fail" but teach you something valuable are more valuable than AI initiatives that "succeed" but teach you nothing about scaling.

The goal isn't to get AI right on the first try.

**The goal is to build the learning curve that compounds.**

---

## The Question This Raises

You have two paths forward:

**Path A:** Wait for more proof, demand guaranteed ROI, avoid visible mistakes
→ 37% success rate
→ Arrive late to a learning curve that compounds
→ Optimize for not failing

**Path B:** Start with learning goals, accept some failures as tuition, optimize for institutional knowledge
→ 80% success rate
→ Build compounding advantage
→ Optimize for learning faster than competitors

**Which path are you on?**

And if you're on Path A, what would it take to shift to Path B?

---

## The Starting Point

You don't need to overhaul your entire approach.

You just need to add one question to your next AI initiative:

**"What will we learn from this—whether it succeeds or fails?"**

If you can't answer that clearly, you're on the 37% path.

If you can answer it clearly, you're building toward the 80% path.

**The gap between those two paths?**

It's not budget. It's not technology.

It's clarity about what you're trying to learn.

**And that clarity starts today.**
