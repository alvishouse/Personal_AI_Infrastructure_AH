A large retailer spent $680,000 on AI over four years.

Total successful deployments: Zero.

Let me tell you what went wrong—because I'm watching leaders make the exact same mistake right now.

---

Over four years, this retailer attempted 15 separate AI proofs-of-concept.

Each one was carefully scoped to minimize risk.
Each one followed procurement best practices.
Each one engaged established vendors with proven solutions.

Every single decision looked "prudent" in the moment.

And every single one failed to generate a deployable AI capability.

**Here's what the "prudent" approach actually produced:**

Four years and $680,000 to learn nothing—while competitors built insurmountable advantages.

---

## What Actually Went Wrong

The retailer wasn't failing because they chose bad vendors.

They were failing because they optimized for the wrong thing.

Every pilot was designed to answer one question: "Does this work?"

That's the wrong question.

**The right question is: "What did we learn?"**

Traditional pilots treat failure as loss. You spend money, the pilot doesn't work, you write it off and move on.

But here's what actually happened with those 15 failed pilots:

→ They learned which vendors overpromise
→ They learned which internal teams resist adoption
→ They learned which data quality issues block AI
→ They learned which stakeholders need early buy-in

**That's $680,000 worth of institutional knowledge.**

But because they optimized for "does it work" instead of "what did we learn," they never captured that knowledge.

Each new pilot started from zero. Same mistakes. Different vendor.

---

## What Changed

The retailer eventually succeeded with AI.

But only after abandoning the cautious approach and adopting something very close to what Singapore is now doing at national scale:

**The Grow-With Model:**
→ Smaller investments in promising partners (not just proven giants)
→ Explicit learning goals (not just ROI goals)
→ Fast cycles (90 days, not 18 months)
→ Accepted failures (reframed as tuition)

Six months after this shift, they had three deployed AI capabilities.

Not three more pilots. Three production systems.

**The difference?**

They stopped waiting for vendors to prove themselves elsewhere. They started partnering with emerging players and building proof together.

They stopped treating failure as loss. They started treating failure as data.

---

## The Tragedy You Can Still Avoid

The retailer's AI capabilities are working now.

But they can't get back the four years.

Four years on a learning curve their competitors started climbing in year one.

**According to McKinsey's 2024 research, AI learning curves run 2-4 years.**

An organization that starts in 2026 doesn't just have a one-year head start on an organization that starts in 2027.

By year three, the early starter might be three years ahead in effective capability.

By year five, the gap could be insurmountable.

The retailer started their real AI learning curve four years late.

Their competitors aren't just ahead. They're ahead *on a curve that compounds*.

---

## The Question for You

If you're a director or executive who's been watching AI closely but moving cautiously, here's what I want you to ask:

**How many of your "prudent" AI decisions are actually building toward capability—and how many are just expensive ways to delay learning?**

The "safe" approach didn't reduce the retailer's risk.

It concentrated risk into a single catastrophic outcome: spending four years to learn nothing while competitors built advantages.

**Here's the uncomfortable truth:**

In AI economics, the cautious path and the failing path are the same path.

The math punishes waiting. The math rewards learning.

You don't need a billion dollars like Singapore. You don't need to bet the company.

You just need to stop optimizing for "avoiding visible mistakes" and start optimizing for "learning faster than competitors."

The 2-year head start you're looking for doesn't come from finding the perfect vendor.

It comes from starting the learning curve now.

**The question isn't whether you'll make AI mistakes.**

**The question is whether you'll learn from them faster than the retailer did.**
