Your team isn't understaffed. It's outgunned.
There's a four-step fix.

You're doing everything right. The hours. The late nights. The heroic effort to keep pace with a volume your team was not built for.

And the companies pulling ahead didn't hire more people to get there. They changed what each person carries into the work.

McKinsey measured the gap in 2025: early AI adopters are handling 55% more work volume. Laggards are handling 25% more. Both groups added headcount. Only one group added intelligence per person.

That is the Intelligence-Per-Person framework. Four steps. Built for operations teams that are underwater and can't get budget approval for more staff.

Here's how it works.

---

Step 1: Audit the Repetition Tax

Every operations team has one. It's the invisible tax you pay every day—hours spent on tasks that follow the same pattern, require the same steps, produce the same output.

Most leaders think the Repetition Tax is 20% of their team's time. When they actually track it, the number is 40-60%.

That's the first problem. You can't fix what you haven't measured.

Spend one week tracking where your team's hours actually go. Categorize each task: judgment-required or pattern-based. What you find will change how you see the problem.

Step 2: Redirect the Headcount Budget

This is where the CFO becomes your ally instead of your obstacle.

Stop asking for new headcount. Ask to redirect the salary of the unfilled position the CFO won't approve. That one move changes the entire conversation.

"I need five analysts" hits a wall. "I can deliver more output at the same cost by redirecting this position's budget into an intelligence layer" opens a door.

One $75K salary redirected into the right intelligence layer can produce what three analysts used to cover. The CFO cares about output per dollar. Give them that math.

Bain's research backs the economics: intelligence-driven automation delivers 18% cost savings. Simple automation delivers 9%. The difference is layering intelligence on top of operations, not just automating individual tasks.

Step 3: Layer Intelligence Onto Existing Operations

This is not a rip-and-replace. Your ERP stays. Your CRM stays. Your existing workflows stay.

Think about how electricity changed manufacturing. Nobody tore down the factory. They added appliances that plugged into existing infrastructure. The output per worker changed completely—without changing the workers.

That's the layer. You add intelligence on top of what already exists. Your team stops spending half their day moving data from one place to another. They start spending it on the decisions that actually require their judgment.

People who were data processors become decision makers. That shift is the advantage.

Step 4: Compound the Advantage

The first layer saves money. You document that. You take the results back to the CFO. You request expansion.

Each layer compounds the last one. The savings from layer one fund layer two. Layer two makes layer one smarter. The whole system gets better as you go.

Meanwhile, your competitor is still adding headcount to solve volume problems. They're on a linear path. You're on an exponential one.

That gap between 55% and 25%? That's what compounding looks like after 24 months of this approach.

---

The window is still open. Only 6% of organizations have achieved the kind of EBIT impact that makes AI irreversible inside their business. Most companies are still in the early innings.

The teams that start now will be two compound cycles ahead of the ones that wait.

Which of the four steps is your team closest to being ready for?
