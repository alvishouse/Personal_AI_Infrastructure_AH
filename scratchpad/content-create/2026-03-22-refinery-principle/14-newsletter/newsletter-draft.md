# The Refinery Principle: Why Your AI Outputs Aren't Becoming Decisions

An NBER study released in February 2026 tracked 6,000 executives across four countries — and found that 89% of them saw zero change in productivity despite AI adoption jumping from 61% to 71% of firms in a single year.

That is not a data anomaly. It is a structural pattern that has played out twice before. Electric motors were commercially available in the 1880s, installed in factories by 1900, and delivered zero measurable productivity improvement for 25 years. Computing followed the same curve. Organizations acquired the technology, layered it on top of existing workflows, and waited for outcomes that never came — until one company redesigned the process around the tool and captured a decade of advantage. The technology was never the bottleneck. The conversion layer was.

This issue is about the conversion layer — the workflow infrastructure that transforms AI outputs into business decisions. It is where 95% of AI value is either captured or permanently lost. It is also where almost no one is investing.

Here is what the data shows.

## Myth vs. Reality

**Most organizations believe AI project failure is a technology problem.**

Post-mortems cite model accuracy. Budget discussions focus on which platform to license next. Executives ask their teams to evaluate Claude versus GPT-5 versus DeepSeek. The assumption underneath all of this is that better technology produces better outcomes. So organizations invest more in Layer 1 — the model — and expect results to follow.

**The reality: 85% of AI project failures are structural, not technical.**

Electric motors arrived commercially in the 1880s. Factories installed them by 1900. But factory output did not improve for 25 years — because factories bolted electric motors onto layouts designed for steam-era belt-and-pulley systems. The motor was not the problem. The factory design was. Ford's assembly line in the 1920s did not succeed because the motors improved. It succeeded because the entire process architecture changed.

AI is the third iteration of this pattern. Right now, 80% of organizations are deploying AI models on top of processes designed for the pre-AI era. The outputs are generated. No one redesigned the workflow to receive them.

**Why this matters:**

If your Q2 2026 AI initiative is centered on model selection, you are solving the wrong problem. The **Driller's Fallacy** is the belief that the organization with the best extraction technology wins. Rockefeller corrected this in 1872 by acquiring 22 refineries in 90 days. He controlled 90% of US oil production by 1879 — not because he drilled better wells, but because he built superior conversion infrastructure.

**What to do instead:**

For your current AI projects, stop asking "which model is best?" and start asking "what is the workflow between this output and a business decision?" Map the conversion path for your three most-used AI tools. Identify who owns that path and whether outcomes are tracked. That audit takes two hours. The gap it surfaces will be larger than you expect. The [full framework is in the cornerstone article](https://alvishouse.io/?p=885).

## Quick Win of the Week

**Audit your AI budget allocation against the BCG 10/20/70 rule — in one meeting.**

BCG's research on AI impact identifies the optimal resource split as: 10% algorithms (model licenses and API costs), 20% technology and data infrastructure, and **70% people and process redesign**. Most organizations invert this ratio entirely. They buy the tools, prepare the data, and bolt AI onto existing workflows with minimal process change. That structural inversion is the single largest driver of the 95% pilot failure rate.

**The problem it solves:**

You cannot reallocate toward higher-impact activities without first knowing where your budget currently sits. Most AI budget owners have never calculated this split.

**How to implement:**

1. **Gather your AI spend data.** Pull actual invoices or estimates across three categories: model licenses and API fees (Layer 1), data pipelines and compute infrastructure (Layer 2 infrastructure), and process redesign, training, and workflow ownership (Layer 2 conversion).
2. **Calculate your current ratio.** Most mid-market organizations find 60-70% in Layer 1, 20-30% in infrastructure, and under 10% in process redesign.
3. **Set a reallocation target.** If your people-and-process allocation is below 60%, shift at least one FTE or contractor engagement toward workflow redesign before the next AI tool purchase.
4. **Name a conversion layer owner.** A budget line without a named owner does not move. Assign one person accountable for Layer 2 results within 30 days.

**Timeline:** 60 minutes to calculate the ratio, 30 days to reallocate

**Expected impact:** Organizations that reach 60-70% people-and-process allocation reduce AI project failure rates from 95% to below 30% and cut average implementation time by 40%. That is not a marginal improvement — it is a structural change in your AI program's odds.

The BCG data is unambiguous. The budget is going to the wrong layer. The fix does not require new spending — it requires redirecting existing spend toward the part that actually produces outcomes.

## Metric That Matters

**The Workflow Redesign Rate**

Formula: Number of workflows redesigned for AI integration / Total number of AI tools deployed

This ratio measures whether your organization is treating AI as an industrial engineering challenge — or as a software procurement exercise.

**Why it matters:**

McKinsey's 2025 State of AI survey tested 25 organizational attributes for their effect on EBIT impact. Workflow redesign had the single largest effect — larger than model selection, data quality, team size, or executive sponsorship. AI high performers redesign workflows at a **55% rate**. AI laggards redesign at an **18% rate**. That 3x gap is not explained by budget, industry, or company size. It is a decision about where to focus.

**The data:**

Only 21% of organizations using AI have redesigned any workflows at all. The remaining 79% are layering AI outputs on top of processes designed for manual work — and generating exactly the kind of flat results the NBER study captured. The 55% versus 18% redesign rate means that leaders are not just doing more of the same thing. They are operating on a fundamentally different strategic assumption about what AI requires.

Deloitte's 2026 research confirms the adoption gap: worker AI access rose 50% in 2025, but fewer than 60% use AI tools daily. BCG found that AI leaders generate 76% higher impact through superior conversion infrastructure — not through model selection.

**How to use it:**

- **Benchmark:** If your workflow redesign rate is below 30%, you are in the laggard tier regardless of which models you license
- **Action threshold:** If redesign rate drops below 20% as new tools are added, freeze tool procurement until conversion infrastructure catches up
- **Frequency:** Measure quarterly; add one completed workflow redesign per AI tool deployed as your operating standard

A redesign rate above 50% is the clearest leading indicator of sustained AI ROI. It is also the metric almost no AI vendor will help you track — because it does not depend on their product.

## Case Study Spotlight: Same Model, Zero New Technology, 34% Less Downtime

**The manufacturing team that unlocked 34% downtime reduction without spending a dollar on new AI.**

This is not a story about a better model. It is a story about a better refinery.

**The setup:**

A mid-market manufacturer deployed a predictive maintenance AI model. The model was configured to detect early failure signatures in production equipment and generate alerts. The technology worked. The alerts were accurate. And for the first several months, downtime remained completely flat.

The model was not the problem. The refinery was missing.

**What failed:**

Alerts were routed to a shared dashboard. Anyone could see them. Nobody was accountable for them. When a high-severity alert appeared on Tuesday afternoon, it sat next to a low-severity alert from last Thursday and a resolved alert from the week before. Technicians checked the dashboard inconsistently. Response times varied from two hours to four days. Outcomes were never logged, so the model received no feedback. The AI was generating crude at full capacity. The organization had no conversion infrastructure to refine it into decisions.

**The redesign:**

No new technology was purchased. No new model was deployed. The only change was Layer 2: alerts were routed by severity with defined response windows, specific technicians were assigned as owners by equipment zone, resolution outcomes were logged and fed back to the model, and escalation paths were defined for alerts exceeding their response window.

Same crude. Different refinery.

**The result:**

**34% reduction in unplanned downtime within 90 days.** The same outcome was replicated in the customer service context by Roland Berger research: a customer service AI tool with no workflow redesign sat at 14% daily usage. The same tool, after routing was redesigned and ownership was assigned, reached 100% usage and delivered 28% faster resolution times.

**The compounding effect:**

That 34% downtime result did more than improve one metric. It changed the executive team's perception of AI from "a tool we license" to "an industrial capability we operate." The second workflow redesign — demand forecasting — was approved within 60 days of the downtime result. The business case wrote itself.

This is the compounding refinery effect. The first successful refinery produces an outcome that makes the second refinery obvious. Standard Oil consolidated 90% of US refining not by conquering the whole market at once — Rockefeller built one refinery that worked, then used that proof point to justify the next 21. Most organizations that get serious about Layer 2 conversion move from one redesigned workflow to four or five within 18 months, because each outcome justifies the next investment.

**The lesson:**

Before you evaluate any new AI tool this quarter, audit your oldest AI implementation with flat results. Map the path from output to decision. Identify the missing routing, ownership, and feedback structure. Implement those changes in 30 days. The model you already have is almost certainly underperforming because of a missing refinery — not because of a technology gap.
