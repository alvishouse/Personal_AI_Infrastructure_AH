Your agents are not failing. Your architecture is.

And that's actually good news.

You've been blaming yourself for months. Bad prompts. Wrong model. Not enough context. Maybe AI isn't ready for your use case. Maybe your team isn't technical enough. Maybe you moved too fast. You've internalized the failure as a capability problem — yours or the model's.

Stop. The research says something different.

UC Berkeley found multi-agent frameworks failing at rates up to 86.7% in production. MIT found 95% of enterprise AI pilots deliver no measurable P&L impact. These are not statistics about bad AI. They are statistics about organizations deploying capable AI into architectures that were never designed to support it.

The problem isn't the agent. It's the absence of a delegation structure.

Here's what that means for you.

If you're a Six Sigma Black Belt: You already know how to fix this. You've designed controlled processes, measured variation, and built feedback loops. Apply that same discipline to the boundaries where humans hand work to agents and get it back. That's where the defects live.

If you're a Lean practitioner: You already see waste in the handoff. Every unverified output, every manual re-check, every "I don't trust the agent so I redo it myself" is a form of waste created by structural ambiguity, not agent failure.

If you're a VP of Operations: You know this is a governance problem in a technical costume. The agents don't have owners. The outputs don't have standards. The degradation doesn't have a monitoring cadence. These are management problems with management solutions.

The good news: you don't need to become a machine learning engineer.

You need to extend what you already know. Flow Bridges replace the RACI for human-agent handoffs. Drift Signals replace the control chart for agent performance. DARI replaces PDCA for the improvement cycle of agentic work.

You are not starting over. You are applying the same thinking one layer deeper.

Your next AI pilot doesn't need a better model. It needs better architecture.
