You have process for everything except delegation.

That gap is why your agents keep failing.

You have Six Sigma for your factory floor. PDCA for your improvement cycles. RACI charts for accountability. Change management for every software rollout.

But when you hand work to an AI agent? You wing it. You write a prompt, cross your fingers, and check the output manually — because you don't trust it. And you shouldn't. You never gave the handoff a structure worth trusting.

This is the process gap MIT, McKinsey, and UC Berkeley all pointed at independently. They just never named it the same way. That ends here.

There is a missing architecture layer. It's called Agentic Process Architecture. And it's what separates teams who get measurable results from agents from the 95% whose pilots never leave the conference room.

Here's what APA adds that Lean never gave you.

1/ APA treats delegation like a process, not a gamble.
Every workflow that crosses a human-agent boundary needs the same rigor you'd give a handoff between departments. Clear inputs. Verifiable outputs. Recoverable when something breaks. Without this, you're not automating work. You're automating chaos.

2/ APA monitors agent performance the way Lean monitors production lines.
Agents degrade. A credit risk model at a major bank dropped from 95% accuracy to 87% in eight months — with no model changes. Process changes caused it. APA uses Drift Signals to catch this before it becomes a business problem, not after.

3/ APA sequences human and agent roles intentionally.
You don't put a human downstream of an agent without a checkpoint. You don't chain three agents without verifying the compound error risk. Three agents at 90% accuracy compound to 72.9%. That math hurts. APA builds the architecture so it doesn't.

4/ APA gives AI adoption a language Lean practitioners already speak.
Discover, Architect, Run, Improve. The DARI cycle. If you know PDCA, you already know where to hang this framework in your mental model.

The teams winning with AI in 2025 are not the ones with better prompts.

They're the ones who treated AI like they treat every other operational discipline — with structure, measurement, and a system for continuous improvement.
