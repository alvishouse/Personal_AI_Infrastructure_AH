95% of enterprise AI pilots fail to deliver P&L impact.

It is never the technology.

You've watched this happen in your organization. The pilot runs. The demo impresses the room. The procurement happens. And then the results never show up in the numbers. Leadership blames the vendor. The vendor blames the data. The data team blames the process owners. And nothing changes.

MIT's NANDA Initiative studied this pattern across enterprise deployments. The failure rate is 95%. And the common thread isn't bad AI — it's an organization that handed off work to agents the same way you'd give instructions to a temp worker on their first day, then wondered why they needed constant correction.

Here are the 3 most expensive mistakes process leaders make with AI.

1/ Mistake: Treating AI deployment as a project, not a process.
Projects have end dates. Processes have owners, feedback loops, and continuous improvement cycles. When you deploy an agent as a project, nobody owns what happens after go-live. Gartner found 91% of ML models degrade over time — but most organizations have no system to detect it until something breaks visibly.

2/ Mistake: Optimizing the agent instead of the handoff.
Teams spend weeks tuning prompts and models. They spend zero time designing the transition points — where human hands to agent, where agent returns to human, what gets verified at each crossing. The handoff is where quality escapes. And no amount of prompt engineering fixes a structurally flawed handoff.

3/ Mistake: Measuring outputs without monitoring drift.
An agent that passes your quality check on day one may be producing degraded work by month eight. Performance Drift, Capability Drift, and Context Drift are real — and invisible without a monitoring structure. Most teams find out when a customer complains or a metric tanks. By then, the damage is done.

The myth is that AI failure is a technology problem.

The truth is that AI failure is a process discipline problem. You already have the mental models — PDCA, RACI, control charts. You apply them to everything except the system that handles delegation to machines. That's the gap.

Close it, and your next pilot won't need to be saved.
